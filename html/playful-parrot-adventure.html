Playful Parrot Adventure (2021)

One month individual course project demonstrating content learned in game design course centered around object-oriented programming.

outcome

The project is a platformer game based on my pet parrot who loved exploring my room and destroying my things. It also serves as an educational guide to caring for parrots, including the plants, foods (and other pets) dangerous to them. Players play as a parrot who has escaped the cage and is on a house-destroying rampage (as parrots do), navigating through three rooms, increasing in difficulty. Clicking floating objects and foods give players points, health boosts and powerups. Collecting all keys and specified amount of points allows them to move to the next room. There are cats and other, bigger parrots to watch out for who can decrease health. If players make it to the end, they are rewarded with a bowl of walnuts, the favourite food of my pet parrot.

process

I drew a storyboard of how each level would look to solidify my thinking. I also made a tentative UML diagram before coding anything to plan out my code and ensure an efficient coding process. I then coded one aspect of the game at a time so I could troubleshoot easily. I implemented user-controlled game mechanics of moving the character with WASD keys and collecting powerups by both clicking and being physically near them. I wanted to create a sense of exploring the rooms, as if the user was experiencing life through the eyes of a parrot.

It was important for the game to have appealing visuals and sounds with each interaction to enhance player immersion. I spent a lot of time designing the settings and characters of the game and perfecting the different character animations on Adobe Illustrator. I am a visual artist at heart and nothing brings me joy like creating beautiful things.

reflection

This project taught me about the importance of planning and organization when dealing with large amounts of data. The final product used over 20 classes and hundreds of variables, which needed to be consistently structured in order to succinctly write code additions, and troubleshoot issues. In future projects I will create UML diagrams as well as schematic sketches to structure class relationships before beginning to code. It also allowed me to explore the creation of graphics for a sequential experience, with variability for level transitions yet retaining overall thematic and aesthetic consistency.
